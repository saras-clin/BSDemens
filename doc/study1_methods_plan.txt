1. STUDY DESIGN AND SETTING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Nationwide population-based retrospective cohort study using linked Danish health
registries. Denmark is an ideal setting: bariatric surgery is centralised, all
procedures (public and private, ~50% each) are mandatorily reported to the Danish
Quality Registry for Treatment of Severe Obesity (DBSO) since 2010, and the
universal tax-funded health system provides near-complete registration.

Study period: January 1, 2010 – December 31, 2024 (surgery / index date)
Follow-up end: December 31, 2025
Minimum lookback required before index: 5 years
  (required to ascertain baseline comorbidities per NMI)


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. DATA SOURCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- DBSO: surgery dates, procedure type, anthropometric data (operated by SunDK)
    Index date source: DatoPER_prim (DBSO's own clinical record of surgery date).
    DBSO also contains OpDateLPR (the surgery date as recorded in LPR / D_ODTO).
    These two dates should agree closely; discrepancies reflect administrative lag
    or coding differences between the clinical database and the national register.
    Planned data quality check: distribution of (DatoPER_prim − OpDateLPR) in days.
    If ≥95% of patients agree within 7 days → use DatoPER_prim, no sensitivity analysis.
    If systematic discrepancy observed → add sensitivity analysis 7g.5 (see below).

    Reporting completeness note:
    DBSO became mandatory in 2010 but registration completeness likely improved
    over the first years of operation. Evidence for this includes: (a) the registry
    itself was new and clinic onboarding took time; (b) early DBSO patient counts are
    lower than subsequent years even adjusting for procedure volume trends. Incomplete
    reporting primarily affects the obesity comparator pool: persons who had bariatric
    surgery before 2010 would not appear in DBSO, meaning they cannot be flagged as
    previously bariatric-operated and would incorrectly remain eligible for the
    obesity comparator pool. This is an inescapable limitation of the DBSO data
    infrastructure, acknowledged in the registry publication (Winckelmann et al. 2022).
    The match-rate audit (main_build_cohorts()) will flag any BS patients who receive
    zero obesity comparator matches — early index dates are the most likely to be
    affected. If ≥5% of 2010–2012 BS patients have zero obesity comparator matches,
    a sensitivity analysis restricting to index dates from 2013 onwards will be added.
    CONFIRM exact completeness figures with DBSO registry managers (SunDK) before
    citing specific years or percentages in the manuscript.
- CRS / BEF + DOD: age, sex, birth date, migration, vital status (since 1968)
- DNPR / LPR2 + LPR3: somatic hospital diagnoses and procedures (since 1995)
- DCPRR: all psychiatric hospital/clinic contacts (since 1970)
  Reference: Mors O, Perto GP, Mortensen PB. Scand J Public Health 2011;39(7 Suppl):54–7.
- DNPD / LMDB: redeemed prescriptions (since 1994)
- IDLMR (UDDA + AKM + FAIK): education, occupation, income (since 1980)
  Reference: Petersson F et al. Scand J Public Health 2011;39(7 Suppl):95–8.

3. STUDY POPULATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

3a. Exposed cohort — bariatric surgery (BS) patients
    All Danish residents who underwent primary RYGB or sleeve gastrectomy (SG)
    between January 1, 2010 and December 31, 2024, as recorded in DBSO.
    Cohort defined from DBSO (national mandatory bariatric surgery registry, operated
    by SunDK), which covers all public and private procedures and has been mandatory
    since 2010. DBSO is used in preference to LPR procedure codes because it provides
    the authoritative surgery date (DatoPER_prim as index date) and is more complete
    for private-clinic procedures.
    RYGB: GastricBypass_prim = 1 (corresponding SKS codes: KJDF10, KJDF11).
    SG:   GastricSleeve_prim = 1 (corresponding SKS codes: KJDF40, KJDF41, KJDF96, KJDF97).
    The cohort is divided into an RYGB sub-cohort and an SG sub-cohort.

    Exclusion criteria (applied in order):
      1. Revision surgery or any prior bariatric procedure.
         DBSO records ReDo_prim = 1 for all revision/redo operations. This flag
         covers three scenarios:
           (a) Revision bypass: GastricBypass_prim = 1 AND ReDo_prim = 1
               (patient had a prior procedure and the current op is a bypass revision)
           (b) Revision sleeve: GastricSleeve_prim = 1 AND ReDo_prim = 1,
               or GastricSleeveReDo = 1 (sleeve-specific revision flag)
           (c) Conversion procedure: Konverteret = 1 (e.g., prior sleeve converted
               to bypass at this operation)
         Implementation: prepare_dbso.R checks ReDo_prim FIRST in the surgery_type
         case_when — a revision bypass (GastricBypass_prim = 1 AND ReDo_prim = 1)
         is therefore classified as "ReDo", not "RYGB". build_bs_cohort() then
         filters surgery_type %in% c("RYGB", "SG"), which automatically excludes
         "ReDo" and "Unknown" rows.
         Verification: explore_dbso() cross-tabulates GastricBypass_prim /
         GastricSleeve_prim / ReDo_prim to confirm flag combinations are as
         expected. Run explore_dbso(raw) before prepare_dbso() and inspect
         the combinations table for unexpected patterns (e.g., both bypass and
         sleeve flags = 1 simultaneously).
         Limitation: DBSO records the current operation only. Prior bariatric
         procedures performed before DBSO's 2010 start or carried out abroad
         cannot be identified through this flag alone.
         [Protocol reviewer comment: confirm proportion of re-operations]
      2. Dementia diagnosis (ICD-10: F00–F03, G30–G31) at any time before index
         — from DNPR (somatic) AND DCPRR (psychiatric)
      3. Antidementia medication (ATC N06D) dispensed before index date
         (flags pre-clinical or undiagnosed dementia)
      4. Death within 30 days of surgery
      5. Fewer than 5 years of registry history before index date
      6. Emigration before index date

    Note on healthy user / surveillance bias [Protocol comment s]:
      Patients with incipient cognitive decline are likely excluded from BS
      eligibility at the preoperative assessment. This could artefactually
      lower dementia rates post-surgery and must be addressed in limitations
      and in the sensitivity analysis (exclusion of early events, criterion 7g.2).

3b. Comparison cohort 1 — General population (GP)
    Risk-set matched 1:25 to each BS patient on sex and birth year (± 1 year),
    sampled from BEF (annual January 1 population snapshots).

    Matching procedure (implemented in 01_build_cohorts.R, build_gp_comparator()):
      For each BS patient, eligible GP candidates must satisfy all of the following
      at the BS patient's surgery date (= the candidate's assigned index date):
        (a) Not be a BS patient themselves
        (b) Same sex as the BS patient (DST KOEN variable)
        (c) Birth year within ± 1 year of the BS patient
        (d) Alive on the exact surgery date (verified via dodsaars death register —
            BEF is an annual January 1 snapshot so mid-year death is not captured
            without this check)
        (e) No prior bariatric surgery: absent from DBSO, or earliest DBSO
            surgery date strictly after the BS patient's index date
        (f) Age ≥ 18 years at the index date. This mirrors the BS cohort minimum age.
            In practice this criterion only binds when a BS patient was operated at
            exactly 18 years of age and a candidate was born 1 year later (birth year
            window ±1), making the candidate 17 at the index date. Rare but enforced
            for consistency.
      N = 25 candidates are drawn at random (without replacement) from the eligible
      pool. Each person can be matched to at most one BS patient.

    Pool depletion and temporal ordering:
      Each sampled comparator is immediately removed from the eligibility pool
      (sampling without replacement, pool depletion). Without depletion, the same
      person could serve as control for multiple BS patients, generating correlated
      risk sets and overstating the effective sample size in the Cox model.

      Under pool depletion, the order in which BS patients are processed matters:
      a patient processed early encounters a full pool; one processed late encounters
      a pool already thinned by earlier draws from the same birth-year strata. To
      minimise artificial match shortfalls, the BS cohort is sorted by surgery date
      (ascending) before the matching loop begins. This mirrors the natural temporal
      precedence of incidence density sampling — earlier index dates draw from a
      larger undepleted cohort. Processing in reverse or arbitrary order would
      disadvantage early-surgery patients relative to later ones for no substantive
      reason. A match-rate audit runs after each matching loop; patients who receive
      fewer than the target number of controls (or zero controls) are flagged in the
      console output and the pipeline logs a warning.

    Bariatric surgery crossover (implemented via bs_crossover_date):
      Pool members who have a DBSO surgery date AFTER a candidate's potential index
      date are eligible controls at the time of enrolment — they were unexposed at
      baseline. If sampled, they are enrolled and assigned bs_crossover_date (= their
      own surgery date). In the survival analysis they contribute unexposed person-time
      from index date until bs_crossover_date, at which point they are censored.
      Excluding them entirely would condition on a future event and introduce selection
      bias — the methodologically correct approach is enrolment followed by censoring.

    Exclusions applied after matching:
      - Pre-index dementia (F00–F03, G30–G31) from LPR2 somatic, LPR2 psychiatric,
        and LPR3 (same three-source query as BS cohort)
      - Pre-index N06D dispensing (any antidementia prescription before the assigned
        index date; same criterion as BS cohort exclusion 3a.3)

    Index date = surgery date of their matched BS patient.

3c. Comparison cohort 2 — Obesity cohort
    Individuals identified by ICD-10 E66 (obesity) in DNPR (LPR2 or LPR3) at
    any time before the BS patient's index date.
    Risk-set matched 1:5 using the same procedure as the GP comparator (see 3b),
    with one additional eligibility requirement.

    Additional eligibility criterion beyond 3b criteria (a)–(e):
        (f) At least one recorded E66 diagnosis (both LPR2 and LPR3 are searched)
            with a contact date strictly before the BS patient's index date.
            The earliest such date across both sources is stored internally
            (earliest_e66) and used solely as a binary gate: does any E66 record
            predate the index date? The specific date itself — whether the diagnosis
            was one month or ten years before surgery — has no further role in
            eligibility or analysis. Only the yes/no presence of a prior E66
            record matters.

    Assumption — obesity as a chronic condition:
        Obesity (E66) is treated as a chronic, persistent disorder. A person who
        received an E66 diagnosis at any point before the index date is assumed
        to still be obese or overweight at that date, regardless of the lag
        between the diagnosis and the index date. This assumption is supported by
        the clinical literature: body weight at the level qualifying for an E66
        code (BMI ≥ 30, in practice often ≥ 35–40 in hospital contacts) does not
        normalise spontaneously or durably in the absence of major intervention.
        We therefore do not impose a recency requirement on the E66 diagnosis.
        This approach also avoids introducing an arbitrary time-window that would
        risk differential exclusion across calendar periods (E66 coding practices
        changed over time) and reduce comparator pool size without methodological
        justification.

    Bariatric surgery crossover: same approach as GP comparator (see 3b).
    Candidates without prior BS (criterion e) are eligible at enrolment; those
    who later undergo BS are censored at their surgery date (bs_crossover_date).

    Note: E66 has low sensitivity for overweight; it captures primarily the more
    severely obese end of the weight spectrum. This is acceptable because BS
    eligibility itself requires severe obesity (BMI ≥ 40 or ≥ 35 with comorbidity),
    making the E66 population a clinically comparable reference.
    References: Gribsholt SB et al. Clin Epidemiol 2019;11:845–54.
                Sjostrom L et al. N Engl J Med 2007;357:741–52 (weight trajectory
                without intervention in severe obesity).

    OPEN QUESTION — GLP-1 receptor agonist use in the obesity comparator:
        Should obesity comparators who initiate a GLP-1 receptor agonist (ATC A10BJ,
        e.g., liraglutide/Saxenda, semaglutide/Ozempic or Wegovy) for weight loss
        be excluded at baseline or censored at treatment initiation?

        Arguments for exclusion or censoring:
          - GLP-1 RAs at obesity doses achieve 10–15% body weight reduction
            (Wilding JPH et al. N Engl J Med 2021;384:989–1002; SELECT trial),
            introducing a competing weight-loss intervention that narrows the
            contrast between the obesity comparator and the BS cohort.
          - If GLP-1 RAs themselves affect dementia risk (emerging evidence from
            SELECT and observational studies), an uncontrolled GLP-1 signal in the
            obesity comparator could bias the BS–dementia estimate.

        Arguments against exclusion or censoring:
          - GLP-1 RAs were rare as obesity treatment in Denmark before ~2021; the
            majority of the study period (2006–2020) has negligible GLP-1 exposure
            for weight indications. Excluding on GLP-1 would create calendar-period
            imbalance and disproportionately shrink the pool in recent years.
          - GLP-1s are also used for T2D at lower doses (A10BJ at diabetes doses
            vs. obesity doses); separating indication is difficult without dose data.
          - The obesity comparator is intended to represent the real-world trajectory
            of severely obese persons who do not undergo BS — GLP-1 use is part of
            that real-world trajectory and arguably should remain in the comparator.
          - Censoring at GLP-1 initiation (rather than baseline exclusion) would be
            more principled if the concern is competing intervention, but would
            require a full time-varying drug exposure extraction from LMDB.

        Tentative position: do not exclude or censor on GLP-1 in the main analysis
        (preserves power and avoids calendar-period selection bias). Address as a
        sensitivity analysis: repeat the BS vs obesity comparator analysis after
        excluding comparators who ever fill a GLP-1 prescription at obesity-range
        dose (liraglutide ≥ 2.4 mg, semaglutide ≥ 1 mg) before or during follow-up.
        This isolates whether GLP-1 contamination materially changes the estimate.
        DECISION NEEDED before analysis begins — see Section 10 open issues.

4. OUTCOMES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Primary outcome:
    Incident all-cause dementia — first diagnosis of F00, F01, F02, F03, G30,
    or G31 in DNPR (somatic) or DCPRR (psychiatric) after index date.
    PPV: 86% for all-cause dementia in Danish hospital registers.
    Reference: Phung TK et al. Dement Geriatr Cogn Disord 2007;24(3):220–8.

Secondary outcomes:
    - Alzheimer's disease: G30 or F00 (PPV ~81%; Phung 2007)
    - Vascular dementia: F01
    Note: PPV for vascular dementia and other subtypes is lower; diagnostic
    sensitivity of registry-based dementia is unknown (Phung 2007).

Ascertainment:
    Primary (A) and secondary (B) diagnosis codes from DNPR (LPR2 + LPR3)
    and DCPRR. Date of first relevant code after index = event date.
    G (grundmorbus) codes NOT used for outcome ascertainment (too non-specific).

5. COVARIATES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
All measured in the 5-year lookback window before the index date.

Demographics:
    - Age at surgery (continuous)
    - Sex (male / female; BEF KOEN: 1 = Male, 2 = Female)

Surgery-related:
    - Surgery type (RYGB / SG)
    - Calendar year of surgery (as period: 2010–2014 / 2015–2019 / 2020–2024)

Comorbidities — Nordic Multimorbidity Index (NMI):
    Conditions ascertained through ICD-10 diagnosis codes (diagtypes A, B, G)
    AND dispensed prescriptions (DNPD/LMDB).
    For hypertension, dyslipidemia, and diabetes: defined as ICD-10 code OR
    relevant prescription within the lookback window (GMC algorithm).
    All other NMI conditions: ICD-10 diagnosis only.
    NMI reference: Kristensen KB, Lund LC, Jensen PB, et al. Clin Epidemiol
                   2022;14:567–79. Code: https://pharmacoepi.sdu.dk/nmi/

    Modified NMI for Study 1 (pre-specified):
      The original NMI includes two dementia-related predictors: hospital-coded
      dementia diagnoses F00–F03 / G30 (ICD weight = 9) and dispensed antidementia
      prescriptions ATC N06D (prescription weight = 11). For Study 1, where incident
      dementia is the primary outcome, retaining these predictors in the baseline NMI
      would introduce circular confounding. Persons who later develop dementia are, by
      definition, more likely to have pre-index register evidence of early dementia —
      the very diagnoses and prescriptions captured by these two predictors. This
      creates a spurious correlation between the baseline NMI score and the outcome
      that reflects outcome ascertainment, not true baseline comorbidity.

      Additionally, mild cognitive impairment and early dementia may be recorded in
      LPR or LMDB months to years before a formal dementia diagnosis is registered,
      meaning the predictors capture early-stage disease that should not be treated
      as a covariate (it IS the disease process of interest).

      For Study 1, a modified NMI is therefore pre-specified that excludes dx_F00_G30
      and rx_N06D, yielding a 48-predictor score. This modification has no impact on
      the study population because N06D users are already excluded by criterion #3
      (antidementia medication as exclusion criterion); the practical effect is the
      removal of the dementia ICD predictor dx_F00_G30 only. The modified score retains
      full validity as a baseline comorbidity summary for all non-dementia conditions.
      Documented in doc/TODO.txt MINOR-4.

    Full condition list (see 00_variables_dictionary.txt for ICD and ATC codes):
      Cardiovascular: MI, stroke/TIA, PAD, IHD, heart failure, AF, hypertension
      Metabolic:      diabetes (any type), dyslipidemia, thyroid, gout
      Respiratory:    COPD, asthma
      Gastrointestinal: liver disease, peptic ulcer, IBD, diverticular disease
      Renal:          chronic kidney disease
      Urological:     prostate disorders (men only)
      Musculoskeletal: connective tissue disease, osteoporosis
      Mental health:  depression, bipolar disorder
      Neurological:   Parkinson's, epilepsy, MS, migraine, peripheral neuropathy
      Oncological:    cancer, anemia, HIV
      Sensory:        vision disorders, hearing disorders
    NMI count (nmi_count, for Table 1 descriptives): number of conditions present —
      0 / 1 / 2 / 3+ (simple count across 33 GMC conditions).
    NMI score (nmi_score, for Cox models): Kristensen 2022 weighted continuous index —
      sum of component weights across the 48 active predictors (modified for Study 1).

Medications (any dispensing in lookback window):
    - Antihypertensive: ATC C02, C03, C07–C09
    - Lipid-lowering: C10
    - Antidiabetic: A10 (A10A = insulin only)
    - Antidepressant: N06A
    - Antidementia: N06D (pre-surgery dispensing is an exclusion criterion;
      antidementia flag also extracted for Table 1 baseline characterisation)

Socioeconomic position (SEP):
    [Not in protocol draft 1; added per reviewer comment p and SEPLINE guideline]
    Measured in the year before surgery. Per Hjorth CF et al. SEPLINE.
    Clin Epidemiol 2025;17:593–624.

    Three separate dimensions are entered as covariates. No composite SEP
    variable is used; SEPLINE explicitly advises against composites because
    education, income, and occupation represent distinct causal pathways.

    - Education: HFAUDD from UDDA — highest attained qualification, most recent
        record up to the year before surgery (event-based register; last record
        before index year used, not restricted to the index year).
        Short (HFAUDD 10, 15) / Medium (20, 30, 35) / Long (40–80) / Unknown (90)

    - Household income: FAMAEKVIVADISP_13 from FAIK, linked via FAMILIE_ID in BEF.
        3-year average equivalised disposable household income (years −1, −2, −3
        before surgery) to reduce year-to-year volatility.
        Quintile assignment uses population-level cutpoints (Q20/Q40/Q60/Q80
        percentile boundaries) computed from the full Danish population (BEF),
        stratified by sex and 5-year age group for the reference year (year −1).
        Statistics Denmark does not publish pre-computed quintile tables at this
        level of stratification; cutpoints are computed from BEF × FAIK register data.
        Quintile categories: Low (Q1) / Medium (Q2–Q4) / High (Q5) / Unknown

    - Occupation: SOCIO13 from AKM, year before surgery.
        Working / Unemployed / Outside workforce / Retired / Student / Unknown

    SEP is a known dementia risk factor (education in particular: lower education
    is consistently associated with higher dementia incidence) and a potential
    confounder for the BS–dementia association. Its inclusion strengthens causal
    inference and is consistent with SEPLINE recommendations for Danish register
    epidemiology studies.

7. STATISTICAL ANALYSES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

7a. Baseline characteristics (Table 1)
    By cohort: BS total / RYGB / SG / GP comparator / Obesity comparator.
    - Continuous: median (IQR)
    - Categorical: n (%)
    - Standardised mean differences (SMD) — no p-values in Table 1.

7b. Incidence rates
    Crude IR per 100 person-years (95% CI, exact Poisson method).
    Reported by: cohort, outcome type, sex, age group (<50 / ≥50), diabetes status.
    Also: 1-year and 5-year IRs separately (landmark approach).

7c. Main survival analysis — Cox proportional hazards regression
    Time = days since index date.

    Three primary comparisons:
      (i)  BS total vs GP comparator
      (ii) BS total vs obesity comparator
      (iii) RYGB vs SG (within BS cohort; SG as reference)

    Adjustment models:
      Model 1: Unadjusted
      Model 2: Age (continuous) + sex
      Model 3: Model 2 + nmi_score (Kristensen 2022 weighted continuous index,
               48 predictors) + SEP (education_cat, income_cat, occupation_cat)
               + surgery year period

    Proportional hazards (PH) assumption:
      Assessed by log-log plots and Schoenfeld residuals.
      Interpretation note: since the PH assumption may not hold over the full
      follow-up, HRs are reported as weighted averages of time-varying hazard
      rate ratios. Reference: Stensrud MJ, Hernán MA. JAMA 2020;323(14):1401–2.

7d. Competing risk analysis — pre-specified framework

    Death is a competing event for dementia: a person who dies before developing
    dementia can never be observed to do so, but their death is not statistically
    independent of their dementia risk (frailty, cardiovascular disease, and other
    factors predict both outcomes). Treating death as independent censoring in a
    standard Cox model is therefore an assumption that requires scrutiny, especially
    because BS may substantially reduce competing mortality, which would differentially
    remove high-risk individuals from the comparator groups faster than from the BS
    group and distort the observed hazard ratio.

    Two complementary approaches are pre-specified:

    PRIMARY: Cause-specific hazard model (standard Cox, treating death as censoring)
      This is the primary pre-specified analysis for all Cox regression in section 7c.
      The cause-specific hazard estimates the instantaneous rate of dementia among
      persons who are still alive and dementia-free at each moment — the etiologic
      question: does BS change the biological process leading to dementia?
      This approach is the standard in the dementia register literature and facilitates
      direct comparison with prior studies. It is valid even in the presence of
      competing risks as long as the cause-specific HR is interpreted correctly (as a
      rate ratio among survivors, not as an effect on absolute risk).
      Limitation: if BS markedly reduces competing mortality, survivors in the BS
      group at late time points may be a selected healthy subgroup, so the long-term
      cause-specific HR may underestimate the true preventive effect.

    SECONDARY: Subdistribution hazard model (Fine-Gray)
      The subdistribution hazard estimates the effect of BS on the cumulative incidence
      of dementia in the full at-risk population, keeping deceased persons in the risk
      set as a permanent influence on the subdistribution hazard. This addresses the
      public health question: does BS reduce the absolute population burden of dementia,
      accounting for competing mortality?
      The Fine-Gray subdistribution HR will diverge from the cause-specific HR when BS
      materially affects competing mortality. The direction is informative: if BS reduces
      both mortality and dementia, the Fine-Gray HR will be closer to the null (less
      protective-appearing) than the cause-specific HR, because the competing risk
      removal of high-risk decedents is adjusted for.
      Reference: Fine JP, Gray RJ. J Am Stat Assoc 1999;94:496–509.
                 Lau B et al. Epidemiology 2009;20:521–525 (interpretation guidance).

    GRAPHICAL SUMMARY: Cumulative incidence functions (CIF) plotted using the
      Aalen-Johansen estimator for all three comparisons (BS vs GP, BS vs obesity,
      RYGB vs SG). The Aalen-Johansen CIF is the correct non-parametric curve under
      competing risks; 1 − Kaplan-Meier overstates cumulative incidence when competing
      events are present (Gooley TA et al. Stat Med 1999;18:695–706).

    REPORTING: Table 3 presents both cause-specific HR (Cox) and subdistribution HR
      (Fine-Gray) side-by-side. Large divergence between the two estimates would
      indicate that competing mortality materially confounds the dementia comparison
      and would be discussed in the manuscript.

7e. Time-period analyses
    Landmark analyses at 1 year and 5 years post-surgery:
    - [0, 1 year]: early post-surgical period
    - (1 year, end of follow-up]: long-term period
    These assess whether the BS-dementia association changes over time, as
    early events may reflect unmasking of pre-existing dementia (surveillance
    bias), while later events reflect true incident disease.

      - Baseline diabetes status (T1D / T2D / No diabetes)
      - Surgery type (RYGB / SG, within BS vs GP comparison)

    6. Negative control outcome — cataract [Protocol reviewer comment v].
       IMPLEMENTED: date_cataract extracted in 02 (extract_negative_controls());
       cataract_event and follow_up_days_cataract computed in 04.

       Outcome: incident cataract (ICD-10 H25: age-related cataract;
         H26: other cataract). Diagnosed in hospital register (LPR2 + LPR3),
         diagtypes A+B, first contact after index date per person.
         H27 (other lens disorders) and H28 (cataract in diseases classified
         elsewhere, typically diabetic cataract) excluded: H28 lies on a
         metabolic pathway shared with obesity and could be affected by BS.

       Rationale for cataract: lens opacification has no biologically plausible
         pathway from bariatric surgery. The established risk factors for
         cataract — age, cumulative UV exposure, smoking history, and genetic
         predisposition — are not materially altered by bariatric surgery or
         the weight loss it produces. Bariatric surgery does not affect intraocular
         pressure, lens metabolism, or oxidative stress in the lens in ways that
         would be expected to change cataract incidence. If the study population
         shares unmeasured confounders between BS exposure and dementia risk
         (e.g. unmeasured SES, healthcare-seeking behaviour, or surveillance
         intensity), these same confounders would also be expected to produce a
         non-null HR for cataract. An HR near 1.0 for cataract therefore supports
         that the main dementia estimate is not substantially driven by such bias.

       Why fracture was considered and rejected:
         Bone fracture was an obvious candidate because it is common, ascertained
         in LPR, and not on the causal pathway to dementia. However, RYGB
         specifically and substantially increases fracture risk through calcium
         and Vitamin D malabsorption, secondary hyperparathyroidism, and
         accelerated bone loss following rapid weight loss. This effect is
         well-documented in the bariatric surgery literature (e.g. Nakamura KM
         et al. Obes Surg 2014; Rousseau C et al. Obes Surg 2016) and is
         considered a recognised complication of RYGB in clinical guidelines.
         SG has lower malabsorption but is not free of this effect. Because
         bariatric surgery has a true biological effect on fracture risk, a
         non-null HR for fracture in the BS vs GP comparison cannot be
         interpreted as evidence of bias — it would be uninterpretable as a
         negative control result. Fracture was therefore excluded.

       Interpretation: HR ≈ 1.0 for cataract supports internal validity of the
         main dementia analysis. HR ≠ 1.0 would prompt investigation of
         confounding or surveillance differential.

    Note — removed pre-specified sensitivity analyses:
    (i)   N06D exclusion at baseline: this is now a main-analysis exclusion
          criterion. A sensitivity analysis on top of an exclusion criterion
          would have no comparison group.
    (ii)  Population income quintiles: this is now the main analysis (SEPLINE
          approach in 03_extract_ses.R). No within-cohort quintile version exists.
    (iii) 7g.5 (OpDateLPR index date): not triggered — date quality check showed
          no systematic discrepancy (see item 5 above).

Implementation note for sensitivity analyses and subgroup analyses:
    Use lapply() + data.table::rbindlist() to loop across outcomes/models rather
    than copy-pasting analysis blocks. Pattern (recommended by tagteam/RDST guide):
      results <- rbindlist(lapply(outcomes, function(outcome) {
        fit <- coxph(Surv(time, event) ~ exposure + covariates, data = df)
        tidy_result(fit, outcome_label = outcome)
      }))
    Apply to: all 6 sensitivity analyses, all 3 comparison pairs, secondary outcomes.

Software: R (≥ 4.3). Key packages: survival, cmprsk, ggplot2, tableone, Publish.
Significance: α = 0.05 two-sided. No correction for multiple comparisons across
pre-specified secondary outcomes.

LIMITATIONS TO DISCUSS IN PAPER:
  - No hospital sector stratification: approximately 50% of Danish bariatric
    surgery is performed in private hospitals. Private patients tend to differ
    from public patients in socioeconomic position. A pre-specified sensitivity
    analysis restricting to public-hospital procedures (7g.3) was planned but
    could not be performed because the DBSO data delivered by SunDK does not
    include a hospital sector indicator. The impact of this is likely limited,
    as all Cox models adjust for three dimensions of SES (education, income,
    and occupation per SEPLINE), which accounts for much of the systematic
    difference between private and public patients.
  - Surveillance bias / healthy user effect: patients with incipient dementia
    are likely screened out at BS assessment, lowering measured dementia risk
    post-surgery. Partially addressed by sensitivity analysis 7g.2.
  - E66 sensitivity: ICD-10 E66 has low sensitivity for overweight; the obesity
    comparator will skew toward more severely obese patients, making it more
    comparable to the BS cohort (which is actually appropriate).
  - Register-based dementia ascertainment: unknown sensitivity; PPV 86% for
    all-cause, 81% for Alzheimer's; lower for vascular dementia subtypes.
  - No individual-level BMI data available for comparator cohorts.
  - Income quintiles are population-standardised (SEPLINE approach): 3-year average
    compared against general-population cutpoints stratified by sex, 5-year age group,
    and reference year. This does not remove residual confounding by unmeasured aspects
    of SES, but the relative position is calibrated to the general population rather
    than the study cohort.
  - OSDC diabetes file covers to 2022; patients with diabetes onset 2023–2024
    may be misclassified.
  - Modified NMI (48 predictors): dementia predictors dx_F00_G30 (weight 9) and
    rx_N06D (weight 11) are excluded from nmi_score in Study 1 to avoid circular
    adjustment. This modified version has not been externally validated; its
    predictive accuracy for the adjustment purpose may differ from the published
    50-predictor score (Kristensen et al. Clin Epidemiol 2022;14:567–79).
