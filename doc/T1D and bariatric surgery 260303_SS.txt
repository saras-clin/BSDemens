Type 1 diabetes and bariatric surgery
Introduction
Over the past century, obesity has emerged as one the most significant global health challenges.1 It is well known as a major risk factor for the development of several comorbid diseases, including type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2D), cardiovascular disease and many types of cancer.1,2 Importantly, the obesity pandemic is not limited to the general population but also affects peoplewith type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1D).3 A group, traditionally characterized by a lean phenotype and preserved insulin sensitivity. But with the presence of severe obesity the insulin requirements can escalate dramatically due to insulin resistance and therefore increase the risk of complications.4 For these patients, severe obesity introduces additional clinical complexities and emphasizes the importance of targeted therapeutic options to manage excess weight. Among the different available interventions, metabolic bariatric surgery (MBS) has gained increasing attention as a potential treatment in this subgroup[a].5,6  
MBS  a wellestablished and safe intervention for patients suffering from severe obesity. MBS is associated with beneficial outcomes such as substantial and sustained weight loss6, improved lipid control, reduced cardiovascular morbidity and markedly improved glycemic regulation in people with T2D.7 These distinct effects on both weight and metabolic health, highlight the transformative role of MBS not only as a wight loss procedure but also as a powerful metabolic therapy. As a result, patients often experience significant improvements in their overall health status, a reduced risk of obesity-related complications and enhanced quality of life. [b]Previous studies have demonstrated weight loss in individuals with T1D similar to those of persons without T1D (kilde 6), along with improved lipid control and reduced hypertension. (kilde 5) A review and two previous meta-analyses have reported that , insulin requirements reduced after MBS; however, another study has reported an increase in insulin requirements after surgery[c](5 og 6) . (5,6,7) Unfortunately, the existing studies are limited in number, and limited by a small sample size (5 to 10 patients). (7). Thus, current evidence on the impact of MBS in patients with T1D is limited with respect to surgical safety, postoperative complications, insulin requirements and long-term glycemic regulation. Therefore, we conducted a cohort study of all Danish patients with T1D undergoing MBS from 2010 to 2024 and compared their outcomes of MBS[d][e][f] to two comparison cohorts: patients with T2D undergoing MBS and patients without diabetes undergoing MBS. 
METHODS
Design and setting
We conducted a retrospective cohort study based on nationwide register data. The Danish health care system is nationwide, accessible for everyone, and tax-paid, which makes it a valuable tool for epidemiological research8. After individual evaluation, the Danish state offers free of charge bariatric surgery to patients fulfilling specific criteria (Body Mass Index (BMI) ≥ 40 kg/m2 or BMI ≥ 35 kg/m2 in combination with obesity-related comorbidity)9. Approximately 50% of patients treated with bariatric surgery in Denmark have their surgery performed at private hospitals8 with self-payment, however; our study encompassed both tax-paid and self-paid procedures. All Danish clinics and hospitals performing bariatric surgery, private as well as public, must report all surgeries to the Danish quality registry for treatment of severe obesity (DBSO).8We retrieved data for this study from the Danish Civil Registration System (CRS)10,{Schmidt, 2019 #22}[g] the Danish National Patient Registry (DNPR)11, the Danish National Prescription Database (DNPD)12, and the DBSO. CRS assigns a unique, civil personal registration number (CPR number) to each resident, either at birth or upon immigration, that makes it possible to link the health care registers for each individual13. The CRS contains updated information on age, sex, date of birth, migration, and vital status from 196810. Since 1995, data from all non-psychiatric contacts in Danish hospitals (inpatient, outpatient clinic, and emergency room), including surgical procedures, coded according to the International Classification of Diseases, Tenth Edition (ICD-10) has been included in the DNPR11. DNPD has collected data on redeemed prescriptions since 199412. In the DBSO, information on patients treated with MBS in Denmark dates back to 2008, with mandatory registration since 2010. DBSO contains information on pre-, peri- and postoperative anthropometric measures, complications, and diagnosis codes.


Study populationWe included all patients treated with SG[h] or RYGB in Denmark between January 1, 2010, and December 31, 2024, who had 5 years of lookback. We retrieved information on surgical codes for RYGB (KJDF10/11) and SG (KJDF40/41/96/97) from DNPR. Subsequently the cohort was divided into three distinct cohorts, the T1D cohortthe T2D comparison cohort, and the non-diabetes comparison cohort.
We defined the two diabetes cohorts as follows: 
 
1. Individuals were classified as T2D if they have at least one purchase of non-insulin GLD, or had a hospital diagnosis of T2D as the most recent type-specific diabetes diagnosis. Exclusions from the T2D population included 1) women with only metformin purchases and any diagnoses of PCOS or purchases of clomifene or antiandrogens and estrogens, 2) individuals with only one recorded inclusion event, and 3) individuals with no recorded inclusion events in the last 10 years prior to the index date[i][j].
2. Individuals were classified as T1D if they had at least one purchase of insulin, or had a diagnosis of T1D as the most recent type-specific diabetes diagnosis, and fulfilled no exclusion criteria. Exclusions from the T1D population included 1) women with any diagnoses of GDM, who have made purchases of GLD only in the period from 280 days prior to their first diagnosis of GDM to 280 days after their last diagnosis of GDM; 2) individuals classified as T2D; 3) individuals without any purchases of GLD, or had made only one purchase and had no hospital records of T1D; 4) individuals with no insulin purchases in the last 10 years prior to the index date.
Patients not fulfilling any of the above-mentioned criteria were included in the non-diabetes comparison cohort. [k]
 
Outcomes
We followed all patients until end of study period, loss to follow-up or death. Outcomes were weight loss at 1 and 2 years, daily insulin dose, hospital contacts [l]due to hyperglycemia, hypoglycemia, self-harm, substance abuse[m], trauma, surgical complications[n], 90-day mortality, and 5year mortality. We retrieved baseline characteristics preoperatively from DBSO. We defined preoperative weight loss as weight loss from the first preoperative examination until the day of surgery. We defined postoperative weight loss as weight loss from the day of surgery until follow-up examination. The total weight loss (TWL) was defined as the sum of the pre- and the postoperative weight loss. We calculated the percentage of total weight loss (%TWL) ([TWL/preoperative weight] × 100), the percentage of excess weight loss (%EWL) ([postoperative weight loss/excess weight] × 100), and excess weight (preoperative weight – target weight [BMI 25 kg/m 2]). We defined reoperation as any surgery due to surgical complications (gastroscopy not included). [o]

Statistical analyses
We calculated baseline and follow-up characteristics as medians with inter quantile range (IQR) for numeric variables (nonparametric testing due to nonnormally distributed data variables) and the number with percentage for categorical variables. 
We computed prevalence ratios (PRs) [q]to compare weight loss and BMI between follow-up visits. We chose PRs to quantify the strength of the associations to base our interpretation of the study’s effect on the size and consistence. Furthermore, we assessed the insulin dose 3 months before index date, at index date, 3, 6, and 12 months after index date. 
We then computed cumulative incidence rates (IRs) of any first-time acute admission and of first admissions due to selected diseases (hyperglycemia, hypoglycemia, self-harm, substance abuse8, trauma, and surgical complications) per 100 person-years in the RYGB[r] and comparison cohorts during the 2 time periods before and after the RYGB/index date. We applied a Cox proportional hazards analysis to compute hazard ratios as a measure of incidence rate ratios (RRs) with 95% confidence intervals (CI), stratified by age and sex, comparing T1D patients with the two comparison cohorts.[s] The proportionality was validated by log-log plots.
