Udvidet formål
Formålet med dette studie er at undersøge, om bariatrisk kirurgi (Roux‑en‑Y gastric bypass og sleeve gastrectomy) er associeret med risikoen for all‑cause demens, Alzheimer’s sygdom og vaskulær demens sammenlignet med både den generelle befolkning og en kohorte med overvægt/fedme. Derudover har studiet til formål at vurdere, hvordan eventuelle risikoforskelle varierer efter kirurgitype, alder, køn og baseline diabetesstatus.
Aim
Sammenhæng mellem bariatrisk kirurgi og demens:
Hvilken sammenhæng er der mellem bariatrisk kirurgi (RYGB og SG) og risikoen for all‑cause demens, Alzheimer’s sygdom og vaskulær demens sammenlignet med både den generelle befolkning og en matched kohorte med overvægt?
Variation efter kirurgitype og patientkarakteristika:
Adskiller sammenhængen mellem bariatrisk kirurgi og demens sig mellem RYGB og SG, og hvordan varierer associationerne efter alder, køn og baseline diabetesstatus?
Betydningen af komorbiditet og metaboliske forhold:
I hvor høj grad forklares eventuelle risikoforskelle af baseline‑komorbiditeter og metaboliske faktorer, som bariatrisk kirurgi kan påvirke?
Tidsafhængige risikomønstre:
Hvordan udvikler sammenhængen mellem bariatrisk kirurgi demens sig over tid efter kirurgi (1 år vs. 5 år), og er den anderledes end hos de matchede kontrolkohorter?

Bariatric surgery and dementia
Introduction
The prevalence of obesity is an increasing problem worldwide1. Obesity is associated with insulin resistance, hyperglycemia, chronic low-grade inflammation and oxidative stress, all known to augment several metabolic conditions and weight loss following bariatric surgery has well-documented effects on the majority of the obesity-related conditions, including type 2 diabetes, sleep apnea, hypertension, dyslipidemia, and reduced mortality.3-6[a] 
Dementia is a syndrome rather than as one specific disease, often defined as any decline in cognition significant enough to interfere with daily functioning. The background for developing dementia is multiple, including neurologic, neuropsychiatric, and medical conditions, with Alzheimer disease being the most prevalent.[b] Chronic fragmented sleep, low-grade systemic inflammation, and oxidative stress may increase the risk of Alzheimer’s disease by disrupting glymphatic circulation, reducing the egress of toxic metabolites and accelerating accumulation of neuropathologic proteins in the brain, including amyloid-ß plaques and intraneuronal neurofibrillary tangles.9,10 Overweight and obesity, as drivers of  insulin resistance, low-grade inflammation and oxidative stress has been reported to be associated with the risk of developing dementia, although causality has not yet been established.7,8
Recent research offers mixed findings on the cognitive outcomes following bariatric surgery, and only few studies have examined the long-term association between bariatric surgery and dementia. Bariatric surgery may reduce the risk of both mild cognitive impairment and Alzheimer’s disease and related dementia.11 Conversely, others have reported associations between bariatric surgery and an increased incidence of Alzheimer’s disease12. Several mechanisms suggest that bariatric surgery may reduce the risk of dementia. Weight loss and metabolic improvements, including reduced insulin resistance and enhanced glycemic control, reduced neuroinflammation, and favorable changes in appetite-regulating hormones such as GLP-1, ghrelin, leptin, and adiponectin, may mitigate neurodegeneration, also independent of the degree of weight loss.11 Combined with these changes, the weight loss-induced attenuation of low-grade inflammation and oxidative stress suggests that bariatric surgery may decrease the risk of developing Alzheimer’s disease[c]. Conversely, bariatric surgery may also introduce risk factors for cognitive decline. Postoperative vitamin B deficiencies, which are linked to impaired neurotransmitter synthesis, may increase the risk of dementia. In addition, substantial weight loss following bariatric surgery is associated with decreased circulating estrogen levels, which have been linked with an increased risk of dementia, potentially through loss of neuroprotective effects[d].12 Thus, the overall association between bariatric surgery and cognitive outcomes may reflect a complex balance between beneficial metabolic improvements and adverse nutritional and hormonal alterations.
Our aim was to examine the association between bariatric surgery and all-cause dementia, Alzheimer’s disease, and vascular dementia. The analyses include the effect of Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (RYGB) and sleeve gastrectomy (SG), both separately or collectively, as RYGB and SG differ substantially in their metabolic and nutritional consequences. By comparing patients undergoing bariatric surgery to both a cohort obtained from the general population cohort and an obesity-specific cohort, this study has the ability to disentangle the potential effect of surgery from the underlying risk conferred by obesity per se.

Methods
Design and setting
We will conduct a retrospective cohort study based on nationwide register data. The Danish health care system is nationwide, accessible to everyone, and tax-funded, making it a valuable tool for epidemiological research.13 After individual evaluation, the Danish state offers free-of-charge bariatric surgery to patients fulfilling specific criteria (Body Mass Index (BMI) ≥ 40 kg/m2 or BMI ≥ 35 kg/m2 in combination with obesity-related comorbidity).14 Approximately 50% of patients treated with bariatric surgery in Denmark have their surgery performed at private hospitals13 with self-payment. Our study encompasses both the tax-funded and self-paid procedures. All Danish clinics and hospitals performing bariatric surgery, both private and public, must report all surgeries to the Danish quality registry for treatment of severe obesity (DBSO).13 We will retrieve data for this study from the Danish Civil Registration System (CRS),15{Schmidt, 2019 #22} the Danish National Patient Registry (DNPR),16 the Danish National Prescription Database (DNPD),17 the Danish Central Psychiatric Research Registry DCPRR,18 and the DBSO.13 CRS assigns a unique civil personal registration number (CPR number) to each resident, either at birth or upon immigration, enabling the linking of health care registers for each individual. The CRS contains updated information on age, sex, date of birth, migration, and vital status from 1968.19 Since 1995, data from all non-psychiatric contacts in Danish hospitals (inpatient, outpatient clinic, and emergency room), including surgical procedures, coded according to the International Classification of Diseases, Tenth Edition (ICD-10), have been included in the DNPR.16 DNPD has collected data on redeemed prescriptions since 1994.17 The DCPRR contains information on all contacts with psychiatric hospitals and clinics in Denmark since 1970.18 In the DBSO, information on patients treated with bariatric surgery in Denmark dates back to 2008, with mandatory registration since 2010. DBSO contains information on pre-, peri- and postoperative anthropometric measures, complications, and diagnosis codes. The Integrated Database for Labour Market Research (IDLMR)holds yearly updated information on the highest level of education achieved and yearly gross income for each citizen since 1980.20[e]


Bariatric surgery cohort
We will include all patients treated with bariatric surgery in Denmark between January 1, 2010, and December 31, 2024[f], who had 5 years of lookback[g]. We will retrieve information on surgical codes for RYGB (KJDF10/11) and SG (KJDF40/41/96/97) from DNPR. Subsequently, the cohort will be divided into two distinct cohorts, the SG cohort and the RYGB cohort. 


Comparison cohorts
We will retrieve two comparison cohorts from CRS and DNPR. First, a general population cohort[h] matched on age, sex and index date wherein each bariatric surgery patient will be matched with 25 comparisons (1:25). Second, a cohort with overweight/obesity (BMI>25 kg/m2 based on IDC-10 diagnosis codes (E66[i])21) will be matched on age, sex and index date wherein each bariatric surgery patient will be matched with 5 comparisons (1:5).[j][k]


Dementia
The primary outcome will be diagnosis of incident all-cause dementia. The secondary outcome will be Alzheimer’s disease and vascular dementia. All-cause dementia, Alzheimer’s disease, and vascular dementia will all be defined on the basis of inpatient and outpatient diagnostic codes22 from the DNPR and the DCPRR.18 The positive predictive value of the coding of all‐cause dementia in the DNPR and DCPRR is 86%, and that of Alzheimer's disease is 81%.23 Positive predictive values for coding of vascular dementia and other dementia subtypes are lower, and the diagnostic sensitivity of registry diagnoses of dementia or Alzheimer’s disease is unknown.23
[l]


Covariates[m][n]
We will retrieve data on baseline comorbidities from the DNPR and the DNPD, according to the Nordic Multimorbidity Index (NMI).24 We will calculate the NMI score for all included individuals. Baseline comorbidity data will be collected five years prior to the index date until the index date and will be ascertained through a combination of ICD-10 diagnosis codes and data pertaining to medication utilization retrieved from DNPD.


Statistical analyses
We will follow all individuals from index date until outcome, death, or December 31, 2025, whichever comes first. We will compute the incidence rates (IR) of the first diagnosis code for dementia, including Alzheimer’s disease, and vascular dementia, per 100 person-years for the bariatric surgery cohort and the two comparison cohorts. We will assess whether the proportional hazards assumption is met, using visual inspection of log-log plots. We will create cumulative incidence plots for dementia, considering death as a competing risk. To estimate the association between bariatric surgery and the first diagnosis code of dementia, including Alzheimer’s disease and vascular dementia, we will use a Cox proportional hazards regression to estimate the hazard ratio (HR) with 95% confidence interval (CI). We will use the two comparison cohorts as the references and adjust for baseline comorbidities. Since the hazards in the cohorts are not proportional, the HRs calculated will be weighted averages of time-varying hazard rate ratios25. We will calculate one[o]-year and five-year [p][q]IRs and HRs to assess the association between bariatric surgery and the outcome. Both IR and HR analyses will be conducted for the surgery cohort in total and for the RYGBand SG cohorts separately. Additionally, we will perform stratified HR analyses for sex (male/female), age (<50/ ≥50[r])[s], and baseline diabetes status using the combined surgery cohort. In the HR analyses, we will compare the bariatric surgery patients with their respective matched controls.[t][u]
