Bariatric surgery and dementia
Introduction
The prevalence of obesity is an increasing problem worldwide1. Bariatric surgery, as a treatment of obesity, is increasing and has proven to be an efficient treatment to obtain a substantial and long-term weight loss.2 Weight loss following bariatric surgery has well-documented benefits on most obesity-related diseases, such as improvement in or resolution of type 2 diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidemia, and reduced mortality.3-6
Recent research offers mixed findings on the cognitive outcomes following bariatric surgery, and only few studies have examined the long-term association between bariatric surgery and dementia. Several mechanisms suggest that bariatric surgery may reduce the risk of dementia. Overweight/obe-sity may increase dementia risk, potentially mediated by low-grade systemic inflammation and oxi-dative stress.7,8 Chronic fragmented sleep, low-grade systemic inflammation, and oxidative stress may increase the risk of Alzheimer’s disease by disrupting glymphatic circulation, reducing the egress of toxic metabolites and accelerating accumulation of neuropathologic proteins in the brain, including amyloid-ß plaques and intraneuronal neurofibrillary tangles.9,10 Weight loss and metabolic improve-ments after bariatric surgery, including enhanced glycemic control, reduced neuroinflammation, and favorable changes in appetite-regulating hormones such as ghrelin, leptin, and adiponectin, may exert positive cognitive effects of the degree of weight loss.11
Conversely, bariatric surgery may also introduce risk factors for cognitive decline. Postoperative vit-amin B deficiencies, which are linked to impaired neurotransmitter synthesis, may increase dementia risk. In addition, substantial weight loss following bariatric surgery is associated with decreased cir-culating estrogen levels, which have been linked with an increased risk of dementia, potentially through loss of neuroprotective effects.12,13 Thus, the overall association between bariatric surgery and cognitive outcomes may reflect a complex balance between beneficial metabolic improvements and adverse nutritional and hormonal alterations.
We aim to examine the association between bariatric surgery and all-cause dementia, Alzheimer’s disease, and vascular dementia. We will assess the impact of Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (RYGB) and sleeve gastrectomy (SG), both separately or collectively, as RYGB and SG may differ in their meta-
bolic and nutritional consequences. By comparing a bariatric surgery cohort to both a general popu-lation cohort and an obesity cohort, this study allows us to disentangle the potential effect of surgery from the underlying risk conferred by obesity itself.
Methods
Design and setting We will conduct a retrospective cohort study based on nationwide register data. The Danish health care system is nationwide, accessible to everyone, and tax-funded, making it a valuable tool for epi-demiological research.14 After individual evaluation, the Danish state offers free of charge bariatric surgery to patients fulfilling specific criteria (Body Mass Index (BMI) ≥ 40 kg/m2 or BMI ≥ 35 kg/m2 in combination with obesity-related comorbidity).15 Approximately 50% of patients treated with bar-iatric surgery in Denmark have their surgery performed at private hospitals14 with self-payment. Our study encompasses both tax-funded and self-paid procedures. All Danish clinics and hospitals per-forming bariatric surgery, private as well as public, must report all surgeries to the Danish quality registry for treatment of severe obesity (DBSO).14 We will retrieve data for this study from the Dan-ish Civil Registration System (CRS),16 the Danish National Patient Registry (DNPR),17 the Danish National Prescription Database (DNPD),18 the Danish Central Psychiatric Research Registry DCPRR,19 and the DBSO.14 CRS assigns a unique civil personal registration number (CPR number) to each resident, either at birth or upon immigration, enabling the linking ofhealth care registers for each individual. The CRS contains updated information on age, sex, date of birth, migration, and vital status from 1968.20 Since 1995, data from all non-psychiatric contacts in Danish hospitals (inpatient, outpatient clinic, and emergency room), including surgical procedures, coded according to the Inter-national Classification of Diseases, Tenth Edition (ICD-10) has been included in the DNPR.17 DNPD has collected data on redeemed prescriptions since 1994.18 The DCPRR contains information on all
contacts with psychiatric hospitals and clinics in Denmark since 1970.19In the DBSO, information on patients treated with bariatric surgery in Denmark dates back to 2008, with mandatory registration since 2010. DBSO contains information on pre-, peri- and postoperative anthropometric measures, complications, and diagnosis codes. The Integrated Database for Labour Market Research (IDLMR) holds yearly updated information on the highest level of education achieved and yearly gross income for each citizen since 1980.21
Bariatric surgery cohort
We will include all patients treated with bariatric surgery in Denmark between January 1, 2010, and December 31, 2024, who had 5 years of lookback. We will retrieve information on surgical codes for RYGB (KJDF10/11) and SG (KJDF40/41/96/97) from DNPR. Subsequently the cohort will be di-vided into two distinct cohorts, the SG cohort and the RYGB cohort.
Comparison cohorts
From CRS and DNPR we will retrieve two comparison cohorts. First, a general population cohort matched on age-, sex- and index date wherein each bariatric surgery patient will be matched with 25 comparisons (1:25). Second, a cohort with overweight/obesity (BMI>25 kg/m2 based on IDC-10 di-agnosis codes (E66)22) will be matched on age-, sex- and index date wherein each bariatric surgery patient will be matched with 5 comparisons (1:5).
Dementia
The primary outcome will be diagnosis of incident all-cause dementia. The secondary outcome will be Alzheimer’s disease and vascular dementia. All-cause dementia, Alzheimer’s disease, and vascular dementia will be defined on the basis of inpatient and outpatient diagnostic codes23 from the DNPR and the DCPRR.19 The positive predictive value of the coding of all‐cause dementia in the DNPR and DCPRR is 86% and that of Alzheimer's disease is 81%.24 Positive predictive values for coding of vascular dementia and other dementia subtypes are lower, and the diagnostic sensitivity of registry diagnoses of dementia or Alzheimer’s disease is unknown.24
Covariates
We will retrieve data on baseline comorbidities from the DNPR and the DNPD, according to the Nordic Multimorbidity Index (NMI).25 We will calculate the NMI score for all included individuals. Baseline comorbidity data will be collected five years prior to the index date until the index date and will be ascertained through a combination of ICD-10 diagnosis codes and data pertaining to medica-tion utilization retrieved from DNPD. Furthermore, we will assess the highest educational level ob-tain, using data from the IDLMR.
Statistical analyses
We will follow all individuals from index date until outcome, death, or December 31, 2025, which-ever comes first. We will compute the incidence rates (IR) of the first diagnosis code of dementia, Alzheimer’s disease, and vascular dementia per 100 person-years for the bariatric surgery cohort and the two comparison cohorts. We will assess whether the proportional hazards assumption is met, using visual inspection of log-log plots. We will create cumulative incidence plots for dementia con-sidering death as a competing risk. To estimate the association between bariatric surgery and the first
diagnosis code of dementia, including Alzheimer’s disease, and vascular dementia we will use a Cox proportional hazards regression as a measure of hazard ratio (HR) with 95% confidence interval (CI). We will use the two comparison cohorts as the references and adjust for baseline comorbidities. Since the hazards in the cohorts may not be proportional, the HRs calculated will be weighted averages of time-varying hazard rate ratios26. We will calculate one-year and five-year IRs and HRs to assess the association between bariatric surgery and the outcome. Both IR and HR analyses will be conducted for the surgery cohort in total and for the RYGB cohort and SG cohort separately. Additionally, we will perform stratified HR analyses for sex (male/female), age (< 50/ ≥ 50), and baseline diabetes status using the combined surgery cohort. In the HR analyses, we will compare the bariatric surgery patients to their respective matched controls.
